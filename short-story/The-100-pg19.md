Peace proved harder than combat. Battle had clear objectives; rebuilding offered only long lists and delayed gratitude. Terraces needed carving before planting season. Water channels required filtration upgrades to bypass old chemical runoff. Shelter expansion demanded lumber quotas balanced against forest recovery targets.

Iris launched education circles beneath a stretched solar canopy, teaching coding and radio repair beside botany and wound care so no one inherited only half the skill set needed for survival. Talan negotiated hunting corridors that protected breeding zones while keeping protein supplies stable. Krell reassigned former raiders to road security under tribunal oversight and labor-based toll systems.

Jonah trained a civilian peace watch with strict limits: body cams during patrol, mandatory incident reports, and automatic suspension pending review after force use. The policy irritated veterans, then earned legitimacy when it exposed misconduct in its own ranks.

Rhea traveled settlement to settlement mediating disputes over wells, language rights, burial grounds, and school access. Some nights she woke from dreams of Helios corridors and ration sirens, lungs tight from phantom recycled air. She kept moving anyway.

One afternoon, children from three factions painted a map mural on the old crash hull, adding rivers, roads, and names in layered scripts. Watching them argue over color choices instead of inheritance of hatred, Rhea felt something unfamiliar and cautious take root.

Home, she realized, was not the absence of conflict. It was a system where conflict could be handled without disposability. They were still fragile, still one bad season from crisis, but they had replaced exile with obligation and fear with procedure.
