# Page 3

Morning exposed how quickly station rules evaporated on open ground. On Helios, authority lived in badges and lock codes. In the valley, authority belonged to whoever could track water, start fire in rain, and keep panic from spreading. Rhea pushed for shared inventories and central triage. Dex rallied volunteers for scouting runs beyond the crash perimeter. Jonah set rotating watches and weapon checks, drawing grumbles from those who thought discipline sounded too much like prison. Iris translated salvage into systems: rain catchers from hull sheeting, battery ration schedules, and a board that listed who took what and why.

Tension surfaced before lunch when three boys were caught hiding antibiotics under a tarp. Rhea nearly swung at one of them before Iris pulled her back and made them return everything publicly. The message was clear: theft now could mean funerals later. By late afternoon they found the missing passenger, alive but concussed, two kilometers downslope.

That night, as cook fires painted the camp in orange, shadows moved at the treeline but never crossed into light. Jonah saw at least five observers. Dex swore there were ten. Nobody slept deeply. Near midnight, a narrow spear landed upright just beyond the fence, wrapped with braided grass and a carved token painted cobalt. No one recognized the symbol. It was neither invitation nor immediate threat, just proof that the valley had witnesses and customs older than the hundred’s arrival. Rhea pinned the token to the map board and wrote their first law beneath it: assume you are being judged.
