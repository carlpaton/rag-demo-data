# Page 8

Work accelerated under the charter because tasks finally had owners and deadlines. Solar cloth became rain collectors. Shuttle plating became reinforced barricades. Iris and Bo Lin assembled a radio mast from antenna fragments and copper wiring pulled from ruined circuitry. If they could monitor Helios traffic, they could anticipate drops or attacks instead of reacting blind.

Rhea assigned mixed crews pairing former rivals to reduce clique control over supplies. Dex hated the policy until Talan beat him in a knife-throw demonstration and volunteered to train scouts in silent movement through marsh terrain. Humiliation made him cooperative.

By dusk, the camp looked less like wreckage and more like a settlement: marked lanes, latrine zones downhill from water, triage corner, grain-drying racks. Children of the younger exiles painted directional symbols on posts, turning logistics into shared ritual.

Then the alarm whistle shrieked from east watch. A Wolf probe had approached within arrow range and withdrawn after testing fence tension. No casualties, only footprints and a second carved threat. Jonah proposed preemptive raids. Rhea refused, arguing retaliation without strategic aim would lock them into endless attrition.

That night Iris captured a faint encrypted burst on the new mast, too garbled to decode but clearly Helios protocol. It confirmed what everyone feared: they were being observed in real time. Before sleep, Rhea added another amendment to the charter board: all intercepted transmissions would be public within six hours. Secrets had nearly broken them once; they would not repeat the pattern, even when truth was inconvenient.
